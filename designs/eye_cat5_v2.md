## The Circle Spotter Challenge

### A. Basic Info

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Activity Name | The Circle Spotter Challenge |
| Activity Category | Collection/Tracking Exploration (Out-of-Device) |
| Recommended Tier | T2 (ages 6–8) |
| Core IB Key Concepts | Connection, Perspective |
| Related Concepts | Identity, Relationships, Discovery, Pattern |
| ATL Skills Focus | Research Skills (observation, data collection), Thinking Skills (critical thinking, transfer), Communication Skills (expressing, listening) |
| Game Style | comparison_chart |

### B. Activity Overview

**① Brief Description**

After photographing their eye (in a mirror or reflection), the AI notices the beautiful circles within circles — the dark pupil, the colored iris, the white around it — and wonders what OTHER things in the world have the same circle-within-a-circle pattern. The child becomes a "Circle Pattern Reporter" on a mission to find 3 things outside that have eye-like shapes: circles, rings, dots, or concentric patterns. Each find gets compared to the eye — is it the same size? Does it have layers like the eye does? At the end, the child sorts the collection by WHY each thing has its circular shape and reflects on how the same pattern appears everywhere for different reasons.

**② Educational Purpose (KUD)**

- **K (Know):** Learn the vocabulary "pupil," "iris," "concentric," "pattern," "layers." Learn that the pupil is a black dot that changes size, the iris is the colored ring around it, and eyelashes and eyelids form protective layers around the eye.
- **U (Understand):** Understand that the same circular pattern appears in many different things — eyes, flowers, wheels, puddle ripples — showing Connection across nature and human-made objects. Understand that different observers notice different things about the same pattern, which is Perspective.
- **D (Do):** Observe and compare circular patterns across different objects. Analyze WHY different things share the same shape. Articulate personal observations about patterns.

**③ Design Highlight**

The "Circle Pattern Reporter" role transforms a walk outside into a pattern investigation. The child is not just collecting circles — they are discovering that their own eye shares a design with flowers, puddle ripples, wheels, and tree rings. The comparison chart synthesis asks the child to sort by PURPOSE (protection, attraction, movement, growth), which earns the Connection concept (same shape, different reasons) and the Perspective concept (what YOU notice depends on how you look). The T2 level allows for abstract reasoning about why patterns repeat in nature.

**④ Typical Scenario**

Child photographs their eye in a mirror, AI notices the concentric circles and launches a "Circle Pattern Reporter" mission to find 3 things outside with eye-like circular patterns, child photographs each, compares it to the eye, and sorts the collection by why each thing has its circular shape.

### C. Interaction Flow

> Recommended Tier: T2 (ages 6–8)

#### Step 1: Transition Bridge

**AI says:** (amazed wonder) "Whoa — look at YOUR eye! Do you see that? There is a dark circle in the very middle — that is your pupil. Around it, there is a ring of color — that is your iris. And around THAT, the white part wraps everything like a frame. Circles inside circles inside circles! Have you ever noticed how many circles are hiding in your own eye?"

**Child responses:**

1. (Ideal) "I see the circles!" / "My iris is brown!" / "The pupil is so small!"
2. (Unexpected) "My eye looks weird up close!" / "It is shiny!"
3. (No response) Child stares at the photo or the mirror.

**AI follow-up:**

1. (delighted) "Circles everywhere in there! Your iris color is like a beautiful ring around the pupil. And here is something wild — that pattern of circles inside circles shows up in SO many other things too. What if we went outside and became Circle Pattern Reporters?"
2. (warm, validating) "Up close, everything looks a little different, right? That shininess is actually a thin wet layer that keeps your eye healthy! And all those circles packed together — I wonder if other things outside have the same kind of pattern. Want to go find out?"
3. (wait 2s) (playful, leaning in) "Your eye has this amazing design — a dark dot, a colored ring, and a white frame. I call that circles inside circles! What if we went outside to see if ANYTHING else has that same circle pattern?"

**Screen:** Eye photo centered with subtle animated rings pulsing outward from the pupil, highlighting the concentric circle structure.

#### Step 2: Rule Introduction + Demo

**AI says:** (adventurous, reporter) "Alright — you are now an official Circle Pattern Reporter! Here is your mission. Part one: go outside and find 3 things that have circles, rings, dots, or round-inside-round patterns — like your eye. Part two: photograph each one. Part three: we compare them all and figure out WHY each thing has its circular shape. Is it for protection? For attracting something? For movement? Let's find out. Ready, Reporter?"

**Child responses:**

1. (Ideal) "Ready!" / "I already see one!" / "Let's go!"
2. (Uncertain) "What counts as a circle pattern?" / "Does it have to look exactly like an eye?"
3. (No response) Child looks around, thinking.

**AI follow-up:**

1. (pumped up) "Reporter is ON the case! Go find something round, ringed, dotted, or spiral-shaped, snap a photo, and bring it back to me. Let's build our pattern collection!"
2. (helpful, clarifying) "Great question! It does not have to look exactly like an eye. Anything with a round shape counts — a flower center, a wheel hub, a puddle ripple, a round rock, even a knot in wood. If it has circles or rings, it is a pattern match! Go find one."
3. (wait 2s) (encouraging) "Here is a clue — look at flowers, look at the ground, look at anything with wheels or rings. Circles are hiding everywhere once you start searching. Go snap one!"

**Screen:** Mission card with "Circle Pattern Reporter" badge (magnifying glass with concentric circles), 4 collection slots (first filled with eye photo labeled "Pattern: Circle in Circle"), and a three-step task list.

#### Step 3: Multi-Round Interaction

**Round 1 — First Find:**

*(Child photographs something — e.g., a flower, a dandelion, a daisy with a round center)*

**AI says:** (eager reporter voice) "Pattern report! Oh wow — I can see round shapes in there. How is this pattern similar to your eye? And how is it different?"

**Child responses:**

1. (Ideal) "The middle is round like a pupil! But the petals go out instead of being a ring." / "It has a circle center like my iris."
2. (Unexpected) "It is a flower." / "It is pretty."
3. (No response) Just takes the photo.

**AI follow-up:**

1. (impressed) "Excellent observation! The flower's center IS round like your pupil, and the petals fan out like a ring around it. But here is a cool difference — your eye uses circles to SEE, but the flower uses circles to ATTRACT bees. Same pattern, different purpose! Reporter Log: 'Flower — Pattern: Round center + petal ring. Purpose: Attraction!' Two more to find, Reporter!"
2. (warm, scaffolding) "It IS a flower, and look closely at the center — see that round part? It looks a lot like the dark pupil in your eye, with petals around it like a colorful iris. The flower uses that round shape to attract bees and butterflies. Reporter Log: 'Flower — Purpose: Attraction!' Two more, Reporter!"
3. (wait 2s) (curious) "Look at the center of the flower — see how round it is? Now think about your eye photo. The flower has a round center with a ring of petals, and your eye has a round pupil with a ring of color. Pretty similar, right? What do YOU think — why does the flower have that shape?"

**Screen:** New photo slides into second slot with sparkle animation; a split-comparison view briefly shows eye and flower side by side with circle overlays. Counter: "2 of 4."

**Round 2 — "Different Purpose":** Child photographs something with a different circular function (e.g., a wheel, manhole cover, snail shell); AI connects the round shape to its purpose (rolling, covering, protecting) and highlights how each circle has a completely different job from the eye and flower. Counter: "3 of 4."

**Round 3 — "Final Find":** Child photographs a third circular pattern (e.g., puddle ripples, tree rings, spider web center); AI celebrates the collection and notes how all circles build from the inside out but for different reasons. Collection complete.

**STUCK BRANCH:** AI suggests looking down at puddles for ripples, at wheels on bikes or cars, at flower beds for round centers, or at knots in wooden fences.

#### Step 4: Celebration

**AI says:** (proud and analytical) "Circle Pattern Reporter, look at your collection! Your eye with its pupil and iris, a [first find] with [its circle], a [second find] with [its circle], and a [third find] with [its circle]. Four different things — all with circular patterns! Now here is the challenge: can you sort them by WHY they have their round shape? I am thinking of four reasons — protection, attraction, movement, or growth. Which one goes where?"

**Child responses:**

1. (Ideal) "The flower is attraction! The wheel is movement!" / Child sorts with reasoning.
2. (Unexpected) "They are all just round." / "My eye does not fit one category."
3. (No response) Child studies the collection quietly.

**AI follow-up:**

1. (delighted, building) "Brilliant sorting! The flower uses circles to attract bees, the wheel uses circles to roll, and your eye — well, your eye uses circles to FOCUS light so you can see. That could be its own category: 'seeing!' Same shape, so many different purposes. That is a real pattern reporter discovery."
2. (warm, probing) "That is actually a really interesting thought! Your eye IS special — it uses circles to focus light and let you see, which is kind of its own category. And you are right that they are all round — but the REASON is what makes them different. The flower attracts, the wheel moves, the ripple spreads. What about your eye?"
3. (wait 2s) (guiding step by step) "Let's take it one at a time. The flower — is it round for protection, attraction, movement, or growth? Now the wheel — which category? See, each one has a different reason for being round. What about your eye — why is it round?"

**Screen:** All 4 photos in a grid; four sorting labels: "Protection" (shield icon), "Attraction" (bee icon), "Movement" (arrow icon), "Growth" (sprout icon). Photos shift under matching labels as child sorts.

**AI says:** (amazed, warm) "Reporter, you made an incredible discovery today. You found that circles appear EVERYWHERE — in your own eye, in flowers, in [second find], in [third find]. The same pattern shows up in completely different things, for completely different reasons. Here is a big question — why do YOU think circles show up so much in nature and in the things people build?"

**Child responses:**

1. (Ideal) "Because circles are strong!" / "Because round things work well — they can roll and focus and attract!" / "Nature likes circles because they are efficient."
2. (Unexpected) "I do not know, but it is cool." / "Because everything is made of atoms and atoms are round?"
3. (No response) Child thinks quietly.

**AI follow-up:**

1. (impressed) "What a thoughtful answer! You are onto something — circles DO work really well. They spread evenly, they roll smoothly, they focus light, and they look beautiful. Maybe that is why circles keep showing up everywhere — they are one of nature's favorite shapes!"
2. (warm, validating the wonder) "It IS cool, and wondering 'why' is exactly what a great reporter does! Scientists wonder the same thing. Circles are special because they spread evenly in all directions — that is why puddles ripple in circles and pupils are round. The shape just works!"
3. (wait 2s) (gentle, inviting) "Think about a puddle — when a drop falls in, the ripples go out evenly in ALL directions. That even spreading is why circles show up in so many places. Your eye, the flower, the wheel — they all use that even, round shape for something important."

**Screen:** Collection displayed with animated concentric ripple effects from each photo; a central "Circle Pattern Map" connects all four photos with dotted arcs; animated "why" labels pulse beside each.

#### Step 5: Closing + IB Concepts

**AI says:** (warm celebration) "Congratulations, Circle Pattern Reporter! You discovered something that scientists and artists love too. You found a Connection that links your own eye to flowers, to wheels, to ripples — the same circular pattern appears in completely different places for completely different reasons. And you brought something special to this investigation — your own Perspective. You noticed things that no one else would notice in the same way, because YOU chose what to photograph and YOU decided how to sort them. No two reporters would build the same collection! You earned your Circle Pattern Reporter Badge!"

**Child responses:**

1. (Engaged) "I love circles now!" / "Can I find more patterns?" / "I want to show someone my collection!"
2. (Quiet) Smiles or nods.
3. (No response) Child looks at the badge.

**AI follow-up:**

1. (encouraging) "Circles are just the beginning! Next time, try looking for triangles, spirals, or branching patterns — they are everywhere too. Every shape tells a story. See you on the next investigation, Reporter!"
2. (warm) "Your badge is saved! Keep those reporter eyes sharp — patterns are hiding in every direction you look. Bye for now, Reporter!"
3. (wait 2s) (soft) "Your Circle Pattern Reporter Badge is shining. What an incredible eye you have — both the one in the photo and the one doing the looking! Great work today."

**Screen:** Golden "Circle Pattern Reporter Badge" shaped like a magnifying glass with concentric circle pattern at center; eye photo in the middle surrounded by 3 collection photo insets. "Connection" in flowing linked-chain letters, "Perspective" in multi-angled colorful letters; gentle chime and animated ripple expanding outward.
