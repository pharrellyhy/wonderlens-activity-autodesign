## Activity: The Secret Eye Detective (眼睛侦探局)

### A. Basic Info

- **Activity Name**: The Secret Eye Detective (眼睛侦探局)
- **Activity Category**: 1 — Sustained Verbal Interaction (In-Device)
- **Recommended Tier**: T2 (ages 6–8)
- **Core IB Key Concepts**: Perspective, Function
- **Related Concepts (Discipline)**: Identity (mapping, PSPE), Emotion (mapping, PSPE), Discovery (designed), Expression (designed)
- **ATL Skills Focus**: Thinking Skills (creative thinking, metacognition), Communication Skills (expressing ideas, active listening), Research Skills (observation)
- **Trigger Entity**: Eye (human)
- **Trigger Scene**: Child photographs their own eye in a mirror
- **Mapping Source**: human_body_eye
- **IB Theme**: Who We Are (mapping: primary, weight=0.61)
- **Dimension Anchors**: imagination (engagement), reasoning (engagement), function (physical)
- **Conversation Anchor Dimensions**: imagination — "If your eye could take a picture of your favorite moment, what would it choose and why?"

### B. Activity Overview

- **① Brief Description**: The child becomes a "Secret Eye Detective" who investigates how their own eye works by solving a series of mystery cases. Each case presents a real-world scenario where the eye does something remarkable, and the child must figure out WHY, using clues from their own observation and reasoning. The game blends imaginative detective play with real optical science grounded in the child's own body.

- **② Educational Purpose (KUD)**:
  - **K (Know)**:
    1. The pupil changes size to control how much light enters the eye
    2. The iris is a colored ring with tiny muscles that change pupil size
    3. Special cells in the eye help the brain tell colors apart
    4. Blinking is a reflex that protects and cleans the eye surface
    5. Eyes work as a team so the brain can judge distance
  - **U (Understand)**:
    1. What you see depends on where you stand, how close you are, and what direction you look — that is **Perspective** (maps to Key Concept)
    2. Each part of the eye has a specific job that helps you see clearly — that is **Function** (maps to Key Concept)
  - **D (Do)**:
    1. Generate creative explanations for real phenomena (creative thinking)
    2. Observe their own eye and describe what they notice (observation, expressing ideas)
    3. Reason about cause-and-effect relationships in how the eye works (metacognition)

- **③ Design Highlight**: The detective metaphor transforms anatomy learning into a mystery game. Each "case" is a real optical phenomenon the child has experienced (adjusting to dark rooms, watery eyes in wind, blurry close-ups), and the child solves it by reasoning about how the eye works. The child is never tested — they are the expert detective filing reports.

- **④ Typical Scenario**: A 6–8-year-old photographs their own eye in a mirror, marvels at the detail, and becomes a detective investigating mysteries of how their eye protects itself and helps them see the world.

### C. Interaction Flow — Detailed Design [Target Tier: T2]

**Step 1a: Transition Bridge — Warm Start** (post-conversation)

> **Context**: Child has just finished a tier_guidance conversation about their eye.
> **Conversation anchor**: imagination — "If your eye could take a picture of your favorite moment, what would it choose and why?"
>
> **AI says**: "*(warm, building on earlier)* You just imagined your eye snapping a picture of your favorite moment — that was brilliant! I keep thinking about how your eye already does something like that every single second. What if we turned your amazing eye into a mystery to solve?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Yeah! What kind of mystery?"
> 2. (Unexpected) "My eye can't really take pictures though."
> 3. (No response) Child is quiet, perhaps still looking in the mirror.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(excited, conspiratorial tone)* A detective mystery! Your eye does secret things you might not even notice — and today, you're the detective who figures them out."
> 2. "*(impressed, thoughtful tone)* You're right that it's not exactly like a camera — but your eye DOES capture light and send signals to your brain. That's actually even cooler than a camera! Want to investigate how it works, detective-style?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle, inviting tone)* Hey, I have an idea — what if your eye had secret superpowers, and we could investigate them together like detectives?"
>
> **Screen**: Child's eye photo centered with a soft golden glow around the iris. A small magnifying glass icon pulses gently in the corner. A faint "conversation recap" shimmer traces the outline of the eye.

**Step 1b: Transition Bridge — Cold Start**

> **Context**: Child photographs their own eye in a mirror with no prior conversation.
>
> **AI says**: "*(amazed, leaning-in tone)* Whoa — look at THAT! You just captured something incredible. That's YOUR eye staring right back at you, with all those tiny details. It's like discovering a hidden world right on your own face! What's the first thing you notice when you look at it?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "I can see the colors! And the black dot in the middle."
> 2. (Unexpected) "It looks weird up close."
> 3. (No response) Child stares at the photo quietly.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(delighted tone)* Yes! That black dot is called your pupil, and the colorful part is your iris — and they both have secret jobs! What if we investigated those secrets like detectives?"
> 2. "*(playful, validating tone)* It DOES look a little weird up close, doesn't it? Most things look surprising when you zoom in that much! But here's the cool part — your eye is actually doing amazing things right now that you can't even see. Want to be a detective and figure out what those secret things are?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm, curious tone)* Take a good look — do you see that dark circle in the middle? That little circle is actually changing size RIGHT NOW while you look at it. Pretty mysterious, right?"
>
> **Screen**: Child's eye photo fills the center of the screen with a gentle zoom-in animation. Soft sparkle particles drift across the iris area. Ambient warm lighting.

**Step 2: Rule Introduction + Demo — The Detective Bureau**

> **AI says**: "*(official but playful tone)* Welcome to the Secret Eye Detective Bureau! Here's how it works: I'll give you a mystery case about something strange your eye does. You think about it, and then tell me your detective theory for WHY it happens. Ready? Let me show you with Case Zero..."
>
> "*(dramatic pause)* Case Zero: Sometimes when you wake up in the morning and open your eyes, everything looks super bright and you have to squint. Why do you think that happens?"
>
> "*(modeling tone)* Hmm, my detective theory is... maybe the eye was resting in the dark all night, so it opened up wide to catch more light — and then BOOM, morning light rushes in and it's too much! What do you think — does that make sense?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Yeah! Because your eyes were used to the dark!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "I don't squint in the morning."
> 3. (No response) Child is thinking quietly.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(impressed tone)* Exactly right, detective! Your pupil gets bigger in the dark to let in more light, and when morning comes, it needs a second to shrink back down. That's your first clue about how the eye works! Ready for your first real case?"
> 2. "*(curious, accepting tone)* That's interesting — maybe your room has curtains that let light in slowly, so your eyes have time to adjust! Either way, your pupil IS changing size all the time depending on the light. Now you know the trick — ready to solve your first real case?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(encouraging tone)* No rush, detective! Here's a hint — think about what the dark circle in the middle of your eye might be doing while you sleep. It gets bigger! And then morning light is a surprise. Make sense? Let's try a real case now!"
>
> **Screen**: A playful "detective case file" card appears beside the eye photo. "CASE ZERO" is stamped across the top in a fun blocky font. A simple animation shows a pupil circle expanding (dark) then contracting (bright) with sun/moon icons.

**Step 3: Multi-Round Interaction — Mystery Cases (3–5 rounds)**

> **Round 1: Case One — The Watery Wind Mystery**
>
> **AI says**: "*(mysterious briefing tone)* Case One: You're riding your bike on a windy day, and suddenly your eyes start watering even though you're not sad at all. Your eyes are making tears for NO emotional reason! Detective, what's your theory — why would your eyes water when it's windy?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Maybe the wind is drying them out, so the tears keep them wet?"
> 2. (Unexpected) "Because dust gets in your eyes from the wind."
> 3. (No response) Child is thinking.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(amazed tone)* Brilliant theory, detective! That's exactly it — your eye has a thin wet layer on its surface, and wind can dry it out. So your eye makes extra tears to protect that layer! It's like your eye has its own sprinkler system that turns on automatically. Case One: SOLVED!"
> 2. "*(validating, building tone)* Oh, that's a great theory too! Dust AND dryness — you actually got TWO reasons! Wind can blow tiny particles toward your eyes, and it also dries the surface out. Either way, your eyes make extra tears to wash and protect themselves. Double solve, detective!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(helpful hint tone)* Here's a clue — think about what wind does to a puddle of water on the sidewalk. It dries it up, right? Now imagine a tiny layer of water sitting on your eyeball..."
>
> **Screen**: Case file card reads "CASE ONE: THE WATERY WIND MYSTERY." An animation shows a cartoon eye with wind lines blowing across it, then tear drops appearing. When solved, a green "SOLVED" stamp appears.

> **Round 2: Case Two — The Color Fade Mystery**
>
> **AI says**: "*(intrigued detective tone)* Case Two: You walk into a dark room and suddenly all the bright colors you were just seeing look gray and dull. But after a minute, you start seeing a little better again. Detective, what do you think is happening inside your eye when the lights go dim?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Maybe the eye needs light to see colors, and in the dark there isn't enough?"
> 2. (Unexpected) "Your eyes are broken in the dark."
> 3. (No response) Child is quiet.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(thrilled tone)* You cracked it! Your eye has special tiny cells that detect color, and they need good lighting to work well. In dim light, those color cells slow down, and different cells that only see shades of gray take over. It's like your eye switches teams depending on the light! Case Two: SOLVED!"
> 2. "*(laughing gently, reassuring tone)* Ha — it might FEEL like they're broken, but actually your eyes are being super clever! They have two teams of tiny cells inside — one team sees colors but needs good light, and another team sees in the dark but only in gray. When the lights go dim, your eye switches to the gray team. Pretty smart for something that seems broken, right? Case Two: SOLVED!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(encouraging hint tone)* Here's your clue, detective — your eye has TWO different kinds of tiny helpers inside. One kind loves bright light and sees all the colors. The other kind works in the dark but only sees gray shades. When you walk into a dark room, which team do you think takes over?"
>
> **Screen**: Case file card reads "CASE TWO: THE COLOR FADE MYSTERY." Split-screen animation: left side shows a bright colorful scene, right side shows the same scene in muted grays. A small eye icon in the center transitions between the two. "SOLVED" stamp on completion.

> **Round 3: Case Three — The Impossible Close-Up**
>
> **AI says**: "*(playful, puzzled tone)* Case Three — this one is tricky! Try this right now: hold your finger really close to your nose and try to focus on it. See how it gets blurry? But things far away are perfectly clear. Detective, why do you think your eye has trouble focusing on things that are super close?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Maybe the eye has to change shape or something to see close things, and it can't change enough?"
> 2. (Unexpected) "I can see my finger fine!" / "I don't know."
> 3. (No response) Child tries the experiment silently.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(absolutely impressed tone)* Detective, that is an AMAZING theory! Your eye actually has a tiny lens inside that changes shape to focus — it gets thicker for close things and thinner for far things. But when something is TOO close, the lens just can't squeeze thick enough! You basically figured out how focusing works. Case Three: SOLVED!"
> 2. (if "I can see it fine") "*(curious, adaptive tone)* Oh, you have strong focusing muscles! Try bringing it even closer — like almost touching your nose. At some point it gets blurry for everyone. That's because the little lens inside your eye can only bend so much. Even detective eyes have limits! Case Three: SOLVED!" / (if "I don't know") "*(supportive tone)* That's an honest answer — good detectives say 'I don't know yet' instead of guessing wildly! Here's the secret: your eye has a tiny clear lens inside that bends to focus. It stretches thin for far things and squeezes thick for close things. But super close? It just can't squeeze any thicker! Mystery revealed!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle prompt tone)* Did you try it? Hold your finger right at your nose and look at it — notice anything blurry? If you did, think about this clue: your eye has a tiny lens that changes shape to focus. Maybe it has a limit to how much it can change..."
>
> **Screen**: Case file card reads "CASE THREE: THE IMPOSSIBLE CLOSE-UP." Animation shows a cross-section silhouette of an eye with a lens inside. The lens shape shifts (thick/thin) as a finger icon moves closer and farther. "SOLVED" stamp on completion.

> **Rounds 4–5 (if child is engaged): Open-Ended Cases**
>
> **AI says**: "*(collaborative tone)* Detective, you've solved three cases already! Now here's a challenge — can YOU think of something weird or surprising your eyes do? Maybe something you've always wondered about? Tell me, and we'll investigate it together!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) Child proposes a genuine question: "Why do I see spots after looking at a bright light?" / "Why do my eyes get red in photos?"
> 2. (Unexpected) "I don't know any mysteries." / Something silly: "Why can't I see through walls?"
> 3. (No response) Child seems done or unsure.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(genuinely excited tone)* Oh, GREAT case! [AI provides a brief, age-appropriate explanation drawing from mapping knowledge — e.g., for afterimages: 'Those tiny color cells we talked about get tired from staring at bright light, so when you look away, you see a ghost-image while they recover!'] Case [N]: SOLVED by Detective [child's implied role]!"
> 2. (if "don't know") "*(supportive tone)* No worries — even the best detectives need a day off! We solved three big cases today, and that's a full day's work." / (if silly) "*(laughing, playful tone)* Ha! If you COULD see through walls, that would be X-ray vision — not regular eye power! But that's a fun idea. For now, your detective eyes have done incredible work with the cases we had!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm, wrapping up tone)* That's okay — three solved cases is a fantastic record for any detective! Let's close the case files and celebrate."
>
> **Screen**: A blank case file card appears with "YOUR MYSTERY" written on it. If the child proposes a case, the title updates. Otherwise, transitions to celebration screen.

**Step 4: Celebration + Role Title — Detective Badge**

> **AI says**: "*(proud, ceremonial tone)* Attention! By the authority of the Secret Eye Detective Bureau, I am proud to award you the title of... Eye Mystery Investigator! You solved real cases about how your own eye protects itself, adjusts to light, detects colors, and focuses. Not many detectives can say they investigated their own body and cracked the code!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Yes! I'm an Eye Detective!" / laughs with pride
> 2. (Unexpected) "Can I do more cases?"
> 3. (No response) Child smiles or looks at the badge.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(celebratory tone)* The best Eye Mystery Investigator I've ever worked with! Every time you blink, squint, or notice your eyes watering — you'll know exactly why."
> 2. "*(delighted tone)* I love that you want more! Keep watching your eyes in the mirror and noticing what they do in different situations — you're building new cases all the time, detective!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm tone)* That badge looks good on you, detective. You earned every bit of it."
>
> **Screen**: A golden detective badge animation fills the center of the screen with "EYE MYSTERY INVESTIGATOR" engraved on it. The child's eye photo appears as a small inset in the badge center. Confetti particles and a subtle fanfare sound.

**Step 5: Closing + IB Concepts**

> **AI says**: "*(warm, reflective tone)* You know what I think is the most amazing thing you figured out today? You discovered that your eye sees the world differently depending on where you are — bright rooms, dark rooms, close up, far away. The same eye, but different views every time. That's the power of **Perspective** — what you see changes based on how and where you look."
>
> "*(building, appreciative tone)* And you figured out that every part of your eye — the pupil, the lens, the tear layer, those tiny color cells — each one has its own special job that keeps everything working together. That's **Function** — understanding HOW each piece does its part. Detective, you didn't just solve mysteries today. You discovered something about who you are and how YOU see the world."
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "My eyes are so cool!" / reflects on what they learned
> 2. (Unexpected) "What's perspective again?"
> 3. (No response) Child listens thoughtfully.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(proud, closing tone)* They really are. And now you'll notice your eyes doing amazing things every single day. Case files closed, detective — until next time!"
> 2. "*(patient, clear tone)* Perspective means that what you see depends on where you are and how you look. Like how the same room looks bright or dark depending on when you walk in! Your eyes showed you that today. Case closed, detective!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle closing tone)* Case files closed, detective. Your eyes will keep solving mysteries for you every day — now you just know how to notice them. Until next time!"
>
> **Screen**: The words "PERSPECTIVE" and "FUNCTION" appear in elegant, bold typography. "PERSPECTIVE" is framed by two eyes looking in different directions with light beams radiating outward. "FUNCTION" is surrounded by small labeled icons (pupil, lens, tear drop, color cell). The detective badge sits below with the child's eye photo. Soft ambient glow.
