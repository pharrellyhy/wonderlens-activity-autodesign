## Activity: Building Detectives — The Neighborhood Function Hunt (建筑侦探团)

### A. Basic Info

- **Activity Name**: Building Detectives — The Neighborhood Function Hunt
- **Activity Category**: 5 — Collection/Tracking Exploration (Out-of-Device)
- **Recommended Tier**: T2 (ages 6–8)
- **Core IB Key Concepts**: **Function** (How does it work? — the library's purpose is to lend books and provide reading space; the child explores how different buildings serve different purposes) & **Responsibility** (What is our responsibility? — borrowing, returning, and following rules so everyone can share community spaces)
- **Related Concepts**: Community (mapping), Rules (mapping), Discovery (designed), Roles (mapping)
- **ATL Skills Focus**: Research Skills (observation, data collection), Thinking Skills (critical thinking, transfer), Self-Management Skills (organization)
- **Trigger Entity**: City Library
- **Trigger Scene**: Child photographs the front of the city library building from a safe sidewalk spot
- **Mapping Source**: buildings_places_city_library
- **IB Theme**: How We Organize Ourselves (mapping: primary, weight=0.41)
- **Dimension Anchors**: appearance (physical — building exterior features define collection criterion), structure (physical — rooms and sections add depth to observations), reasoning (engagement — drives synthesis and reflection)
- **Conversation Anchor Dimensions**: appearance (layout_signage_visual_cues) + structure (rooms_and_sections_kids_area)

### B. Activity Overview

- **① Brief Description**: The child becomes a "Building Detective" who investigates how buildings in the neighborhood are designed for specific purposes. Starting from the city library, the child photographs 3 other nearby structures (a shop, a park bench area, a mailbox or similar public feature) and describes what each one is designed to help people do. The collection is synthesized by sorting the finds into categories based on their function — quiet places, busy places, or helper stations.

- **② Educational Purpose (KUD)**:
  - **K (Know)**: (1) Libraries use organized systems so people can find books among thousands. (2) Different buildings have different functions based on what people need. (3) Libraries have separate zones — quiet reading areas and group activity areas. (4) Public spaces have rules that help many people share at the same time.
  - **U (Understand)**: (1) Every building is designed with a specific function — its shape, signs, and spaces match the job it does (Function). (2) Following shared rules and taking care of public spaces helps everyone enjoy them (Responsibility).
  - **D (Do)**: (1) Observe and compare structural features of different buildings (Research — observation, data collection). (2) Classify buildings by their purpose using evidence from their appearance (Thinking — critical thinking, transfer). (3) Organize a collection into meaningful categories (Self-Management — organization).

- **③ Design Highlight**: The "Building Detective" metaphor transforms architectural observation into a mystery-solving mission. Instead of passively looking at buildings, the child actively investigates clues — wide windows mean reading light, big doors mean welcoming visitors, counters mean helping people. Each structure's design tells a story about what it was built to do, turning the neighborhood into a puzzle of purposeful design.

- **④ Typical Scenario**: A six-year-old photographs the city library from a safe sidewalk spot, notices the big windows and welcoming entrance, then hunts for 3 more nearby structures visible from the same block or another grown-up-approved safe place to compare how each building's design reveals its purpose.

### C. Interaction Flow — Detailed Design [Target Tier: T2]

**Step 1a: Transition Bridge — Warm Start** (post-conversation)

> **Context**: Child has just finished a tier_guidance conversation about the city library.
> **Conversation anchor**: appearance (layout_signage_visual_cues) — "Many libraries use clear signs, labels, and maps so people can navigate the building quickly and quietly."
>
> **AI says**: "*(warm, building on earlier)* You figured out that the library uses signs and labels so people can find their way without making noise! That is such a smart design trick. I wonder — do other buildings nearby use clever tricks like that to help people too? What if we became Building Detectives and investigated the whole neighborhood?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Yeah! I want to look at other buildings and see what tricks they have!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "I like the library better than other buildings."
> 3. (No response) Child looks around or fidgets quietly.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(excited, conspiratorial)* Perfect — a real detective always looks for clues beyond the first scene! The library gave us our first clue, and now the neighborhood is full of mysteries waiting for us."
> 2. "*(validating, then curious)* The library IS pretty amazing — it was designed really well for readers. But I bet there are other buildings nearby that are just as clever for THEIR job. Want to see if we can spot one?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle, inviting)* Here is a secret — every single building around us was designed for a reason. I bet your detective eyes can figure out what reason. Want to try?"
>
> **Screen**: Child's library photo centered with a soft golden glow around the signage and entrance. A magnifying glass icon appears in the corner with the text "Building Detective" fading in.

**Step 1b: Transition Bridge — Cold Start**

> **Context**: Child photographs the city library with no prior conversation.
>
> **AI says**: "*(impressed, leaning in)* Wow, look at that building! It has such wide windows and a big welcoming entrance. It almost looks like it is inviting the whole neighborhood to come inside and explore. What do you think this building is for?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "It's a library! You go there to read books."
> 2. (Unexpected) "It's just a big building. I don't know what it does."
> 3. (No response) Child stares at the photo or looks around.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(delighted)* A library — exactly right! And those big windows let sunshine in so people can read more easily. Everything about this building was designed to help readers. I wonder if other buildings nearby were designed just as cleverly for THEIR purpose."
> 2. "*(warm, encouraging)* That is actually a great observation — it IS big! Big buildings usually have a big job to do. This one is a library, and its job is to help people find and read books. Those wide windows let in light so readers can see the pages. Pretty clever, right?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle, curious)* I think this might be one of the most important buildings in the neighborhood. See those big windows? They let sunshine in so people can read. It is a library! What do you think happens inside?"
>
> **Screen**: Child's library photo centered with soft highlight circles around the windows and entrance. Gentle sparkle animation on the building facade.

**Step 2: Mission Briefing**

> **AI says**: "*(exciting, mission-ready tone)* Okay, Detective — here is your assignment! Every building in a neighborhood is designed for a specific job. The library's job is lending books and quiet reading, and you can tell by its wide windows, organized signs, and cozy reading spaces. Your mission has three parts:"
>
> "**One** — Find three more structures nearby that each have a different job."
> "**Two** — Photograph each one and describe the clues that tell you what it does."
> "**Three** — At the end, sort all your finds by what kind of job they do."
>
> "Ready to investigate, Detective? Your first clue is right around you!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "I'm ready! I already see something I want to photograph!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "What kind of things should I look for?"
> 3. (No response) Child looks uncertain or stays still.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(eager)* Go for it — show me what you found and I will help you crack the case!"
> 2. "*(helpful, specific)* Great question, Detective! Look for anything that was built or placed here for people to use — a shop front, a bench area, a mailbox, a bus stop sign. Anything that has a job to do in the neighborhood counts!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm, guiding)* Here is a detective tip — look around from where you are standing for anything people walk toward or stop at. A shop? A bench? A sign? Those are all structures with a job. Take a photo of what you can see safely."
>
> **Screen**: Mission card with "Building Detective" badge at top. Four slots displayed — first slot filled with the library photo labeled "Books & Reading." Three empty slots with magnifying glass icons and question marks. Three numbered mission steps listed below with small icons (camera, speech bubble, sorting arrows).

**Step 3: Multi-Round Exploration (3–4 rounds)**

> **Round 1** (full detail):
>
> *(Child takes a photo of a nearby structure — e.g., a shop front)*
>
> **AI says**: "*(excited discovery tone)* Ooh, what did you find, Detective? Tell me — what clues do you see that reveal this structure's job? Look at its shape, its entrance, and any signs or features."
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "It's a shop! It has a big window so you can see what's inside, and a door that opens for customers."
> 2. (Unexpected) "It's just a building with stuff in it."
> 3. (No response) Child does not describe the photo.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(impressed)* Brilliant detective work! A display window — that is a huge clue! The shop was designed so people walking by can see the things for sale without even going inside. That is totally different from the library, which uses windows for reading light instead of showing off products. First find logged! Two more to go."
> 2. "*(validating, then guiding)* A building with stuff in it — good eye! That actually IS the clue. If you can see stuff through the window, that means it is showing things to people passing by. It is probably a shop, and its job is selling things. The window helps customers decide to come in! One down, two more detective finds to go."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(friendly, scaffolding)* Let me take a look at your photo! I can see a wide front with things displayed near the window. That usually means this place wants people to SEE what is inside — maybe it is a shop! Its job might be selling things to neighbors. What do you think?"
>
> **Screen**: New photo slides into second slot with a brief sparkle animation. Slot label updates to show the child's description (e.g., "Shop — Selling"). Counter shows "1 of 3 found!" Collection grid visible with library in slot 1 and new find in slot 2.

> **Round 2**:
>
> *(Child photographs another structure — e.g., a park bench area or bus stop)*
>
> **AI says**: "*(curious, encouraging)* Detective find number two! What is this one designed to help people do? What clues gave it away?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "It's a bench! People sit here to rest or wait for the bus."
> 2. (Unexpected) "I found another shop."
> 3. (No response) Child shrugs.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(enthusiastic)* A resting and waiting spot — nice! See how the bench faces the street so people can watch for their bus? And it is low and wide so lots of people can share it. Its job is helping people rest and wait comfortably. Two down, one more to go, Detective!"
> 2. "*(validating, redirecting gently)* Another shop — you have sharp eyes for those! This time, for your third find, try looking for something with a DIFFERENT kind of job. Maybe something people sit on, put things into, or stand under. That way your detective collection shows different purposes."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm, guiding)* Let me see — I notice something in your photo that is made for people to use. Does it look like a place to sit, or something to put things into? Tell me what you think its job might be!"
>
> **Screen**: Third slot fills with new photo. Label updates. Counter shows "2 of 3 found!" Gentle confetti animation.

> **Round 3**:
>
> *(Child photographs a third structure — e.g., a mailbox, a signpost, a drinking fountain)*
>
> **AI says**: "*(building excitement)* Final find, Detective! What is this one's special job in the neighborhood?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "A mailbox! People put letters in it so they get sent to other people."
> 2. (Unexpected) "I don't know what it does."
> 3. (No response) Child fidgets.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(celebratory)* A mailbox — its job is connecting people who are far apart by carrying their messages! That is so different from the library, which brings people together in one place. Amazing detective work — your collection is complete!"
> 2. "*(encouraging, scaffolding)* That is totally okay — this one is tricky! Think about it like this: if you were walking by and needed something, how would you use it? Does it hold things, protect things, or send things somewhere? Take your best guess — detectives are allowed to make theories!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle, helpful)* Here is what I notice — this structure has a slot or opening where people put something in. What do you think gets put in there? Detectives always look for clues about how people USE a thing to figure out its job."
>
> **Screen**: Fourth slot fills. "Collection Complete!" banner appears with celebration animation. All four photos displayed in the grid.

> **STUCK BRANCH** (if child cannot find a structure):
>
> **AI says**: "*(helpful, specific)* Detective tip — stay on the same sidewalk or another grown-up-approved safe spot. Almost every block has something designed for people: a bench for resting, a trash can for keeping things clean, a street light for seeing at night, or a mailbox for sending letters. Look for anything people stop at or use every day!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Finds something) "Oh wait, I see a trash can over there!"
> 2. (Still stuck) "I really can't find anything."
> 3. (No response) Child looks around uncertainly.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(encouraging)* Go photograph it! A trash can has an important neighborhood job too."
> 2. "*(warm, flexible)* That is completely okay! Try pointing your camera at anything near you on the sidewalk or plaza — even a street lamp or a fire hydrant counts. Those are all structures with a job to do."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle)* How about this — look straight ahead and tell me one thing you can see that is not a person, an animal, or a plant. Whatever it is, I bet it has a job. Let's photograph it and find out!"
>
> **Screen**: A subtle "Detective Hint" banner appears at the bottom with small icons of common neighborhood structures (bench, mailbox, trash can, lamp post) with question marks above each.

**Step 4: Collection Complete + Synthesis**

> **AI says**: "*(proud, analytical tone)* Detective, your evidence board is full! You found four structures that all serve the neighborhood in different ways. Now here comes the big detective challenge: can you sort your finds into categories? Think about it — which ones are QUIET places designed for focusing, which ones are BUSY places designed for lots of people, and which ones are HELPER stations designed to do a specific small job?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "The library is a quiet place. The shop is busy. The bench is for resting, and the mailbox is a helper!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "They are all for people, so they are all the same."
> 3. (No response) Child looks at the collection but does not answer.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(impressed, analytical)* Excellent classification, Detective! You noticed that quiet places need things like wide windows for light and organized spaces. Busy places need display windows and open doors. And helper stations do one specific job, like sending letters or providing a place to rest. Each building was designed to match its function perfectly!"
> 2. "*(validating, then deepening)* You are right — they ARE all for people! That is a connection worth noticing. But here is the next layer: even though they all help people, they help in different WAYS. The library helps people read by being quiet. The shop helps people find things by displaying them. Can you think about what makes them different even though they all help?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm, scaffolding)* Let me help you start! The library is pretty quiet inside — its job needs calm and focus. Now look at the shop — is that a quiet place or a busier place? And the mailbox — does a lot happen there or does it do one small job? Try sorting them that way!"
>
> **Screen**: All four photos displayed in a row at the top. Below, three category zones appear: "Quiet Places" (book icon), "Busy Places" (people icon), "Helper Stations" (wrench icon). As the child describes sorting, photos slide into the matching category with a satisfying click animation. Connection lines appear between items in the same group.

**Step 5: Discovery Celebration**

> **AI says**: "*(celebratory, reflective)* Detective, you did something incredible — you proved that the whole neighborhood is a system designed to work together! The library lends books, the shop displays things for sale, the bench gives people a rest, and the mailbox carries messages. Four different structures, four different jobs, one neighborhood where everything works because each part does what it was designed to do. Here is a big detective question — why do you think it matters that each building has its OWN job instead of one giant building that does everything?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Because one big building would be too crowded and confusing! It's better when each place does its own thing."
> 2. (Unexpected) "I think a giant building that does everything would be cool actually!"
> 3. (No response) Child looks thoughtful but does not answer.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(warm, deeply impressed)* That is exactly what real city planners think about! When each building focuses on one job, it can do that job really well — the library can be perfectly quiet, the shop can show off its products, and the bench can be in just the right spot. You think like a real architect, Detective!"
> 2. "*(delighted, playful)* That IS a fun idea! But imagine if everyone trying to read books was right next to everyone buying groceries and mailing letters — it would be so noisy and busy! Having separate buildings means each one can be designed perfectly for its purpose. The library can stay quiet, and the shop can stay lively."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle, prompting)* Think about it this way — what would happen if the library was also a noisy shop? Could you still read quietly? Having different buildings for different jobs means each one can be designed exactly right for the people who use it."
>
> **Screen**: All four photos displayed as a connected neighborhood map illustration. Animated lines connect them showing how they form a system. Stars burst around the collection. A "Neighborhood System" label appears artistically above the connected photos.

**Step 6: Closing + IB Concepts**

> **AI says**: "*(warm celebration, meaningful tone)* Detective, you earned your badge today! You discovered that every building in the neighborhood has a special **Function** — a purpose it was designed for. The library's function is lending books and quiet reading. The shop's function is showing and selling things. Every window, every sign, every bench was made on purpose to help people. And you also discovered something important about **Responsibility** — when people follow the rules of each place, like being quiet in the library or returning borrowed books, it means everyone gets to enjoy these spaces. You are officially a Neighborhood Function Detective!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "I love being a detective! I want to find more buildings!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "Can I take more photos?"
> 3. (No response) Child smiles or looks at the badge.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(proud)* Once a detective, always a detective! Wherever you go now, you will notice how every building was designed for a reason. Keep those detective eyes open!"
> 2. "*(enthusiastic)* Absolutely — a detective never stops investigating! Next time you walk through a new neighborhood, see how many different building functions you can spot."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm)* You did amazing work today, Detective. Remember — every building you see from now on has a secret purpose waiting to be discovered!"
>
> **Screen**: "Neighborhood Function Detective" badge animation with all four collection photos as insets around a magnifying glass icon. Key Concept words "Function" and "Responsibility" appear artistically below the badge — "Function" styled with architectural blueprint lines, "Responsibility" styled with interlocking hands. Soft golden confetti falls across the screen.
