# Activity Design: Bicycle + Category 5 (Collection/Tracking Exploration)

## Activity: Wheel Spotter Safari

### A. Basic Info

- **Activity Name**: Wheel Spotter Safari
- **Activity Category**: 5 — Collection/Tracking Exploration (Out-of-Device)
- **Recommended Tier**: T1 (ages 4-6)
- **Core IB Key Concepts**: Function, Responsibility
- **Related Concepts**: Rules (mapping), Community (mapping), Discovery (designed), Structure (designed)
- **ATL Skills Focus**: Research Skills (observation, data collection), Thinking Skills (critical thinking, transfer), Self-Management Skills (organization)
- **Trigger Entity**: Bicycle
- **Trigger Scene**: Child photographs their bicycle parked in the driveway
- **Mapping Source**: vehicles_bicycle
- **IB Theme**: How We Organize Ourselves (mapping: primary, weight=0.48)
- **Dimension Anchors**: appearance (physical), structure (physical), relationship (engagement)
- **Conversation Anchor Dimensions**: appearance — spoke_pattern, structure — chain_and_gears

### B. Activity Overview

- **1. Brief Description**: The child photographs their bicycle and notices its wheels, spokes, and round spinning parts. The AI frames a collection mission: become a "Wheel Spotter" and find other things nearby that have wheels, circular parts, or similar spinning/rolling features. The child explores the driveway and yard, photographing finds and comparing each one to the bicycle's round, spinning design.

- **2. Educational Purpose (KUD)**:
  - **K (Know)**: Wheels help things roll and move; spokes spread out from the center like a spiderweb; pedals push a chain to turn wheels; many vehicles and tools use round parts; reflectors help others see the bike.
  - **U (Understand)**: Round, spinning parts serve a Function — they help things move or work (Function). When we use tools like bikes, we follow rules and take care of them (Responsibility).
  - **D (Do)**: Observe and compare circular features across different objects (Research — observation); transfer the concept of "round parts that move" to new finds (Thinking — transfer); organize a collection by shared features (Self-Management — organization).

- **3. Design Highlight**: The bicycle's spokes become the visual hook — "a wheel spiderweb" — and the child hunts for anything nearby with wheels, circles, or spinning parts, building a "Wheel Spotter Field Guide" of round things that roll, spin, or turn.

- **4. Typical Scenario**: Child in their driveway photographs their bike, then searches the yard, garage, and sidewalk for things with wheels or circular parts — a wagon, a garden hose reel, a trash can lid, a scooter wheel.

### C. Interaction Flow — Detailed Design [Target Tier: T1]

**Step 1a: Transition Bridge — Warm Start** (post-conversation)

> **Context**: Child has just finished a tier_guidance conversation about their bicycle.
> **Conversation anchor**: appearance — spoke_pattern ("thin spokes spreading out like a wheel spiderweb")
>
> **AI says**: "*(warm, building on earlier)* You noticed the spokes look like a spiderweb! I wonder what else nearby has round spinning parts. Want to go find out?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Yeah! I want to find more wheels!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "My scooter has wheels too!"
> 3. (No response) Child looks around quietly or fidgets.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(excited)* Great! Let's go on a wheel hunt! I bet there are cool round things hiding nearby."
> 2. "*(impressed)* A scooter — yes! That has round parts too. Let's see what else we can spot!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle, encouraging)* Look at those round wheels on your bike. Can you find something else that is round nearby?"
>
> **Screen**: Bicycle photo centered with a soft golden glow highlighting the wheel spokes. A small "conversation recap" tag reads "spiderweb spokes" with a tiny spoke icon.

**Step 1b: Transition Bridge — Cold Start**

> **Context**: Child photographs their bicycle parked in the driveway with no prior conversation.
>
> **AI says**: "*(delighted surprise)* Wow, look at your bicycle! Those wheels have so many thin spokes — like a spiderweb made of metal. What is your favorite part of this bike?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "I like the wheels! They spin really fast!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "It's red! Red is my favorite color."
> 3. (No response) Child stares at the screen or touches the bike.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(enthusiastic)* The spinning wheels are amazing! They go round and round. I wonder what else nearby spins or rolls like that."
> 2. "*(warm)* Red is a great color for a bike! And look at those round wheels. I bet we can find more round things nearby."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(soft, inviting)* See those big round wheels? They help the bike roll. Can you point to a wheel for me?"
>
> **Screen**: Bicycle photo centered with the wheel area softly highlighted by a glowing circle. Gentle sparkle animation on the spokes.

**Step 2: Mission Briefing**

> **AI says**: "*(adventurous tone)* You are now a Wheel Spotter! Your mission has three parts. First, find two or three things nearby with wheels or round parts. Next, take a photo of each one. Then, we will see how they all compare to your bicycle!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Okay! I'll go look for wheels!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "Can I take a picture of my bike again?"
> 3. (No response) Child hesitates, looking unsure where to go.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(cheering)* Go, Wheel Spotter! Look in the garage, the yard, or along the sidewalk. Bring back your finds!"
> 2. "*(playful)* Your bike is already in our collection! Now let's find something new. Check near the garage or the sidewalk."
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(helpful, warm)* Try looking near the garage door. Wagons, scooters, even trash cans have round parts. What can you spot?"
>
> **Screen**: Mission card appears with a "Wheel Spotter" badge (magnifying glass over a wheel icon). Three empty photo slots below the bicycle photo (slot 1 filled). A numbered checklist: 1) Find 2-3 round things, 2) Photograph each one, 3) Compare them all.

**Step 3: Multi-Round Exploration (3-4 rounds)**

> **Round 1**
>
> *(Child takes a photo of something — e.g., a wagon)*
>
> **AI says**: "*(excited discovery)* You found something! It has wheels on it. What is this? Tell me about the round parts you see."
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "It's a wagon! It has four wheels."
> 2. (Unexpected) "It's red like my bike."
> 3. (No response) Child just watches the screen.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(impressed)* A wagon with four wheels — nice find! Those wheels help it roll, just like your bike. Two more to go, Wheel Spotter!"
> 2. "*(warm)* Red like your bike — good eye! And look, it has round wheels on the bottom. Those help it roll. Two more finds to go!"
> 3. "*(friendly)* I see something with round wheels on it! Looks like it can roll around. What would you call it? Two more to go!"
>
> **Screen**: New photo slides into slot 2. A small counter reads "1 of 3 found!" Brief spinning-wheel celebration animation.

> **Round 2**
>
> *(Child takes a photo — e.g., a garden hose reel or a skateboard)*
>
> **AI says**: "*(curious tone)* Ooh, another find! Does this one spin or roll? How are its round parts different from the bike wheels?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "The hose reel spins around! But it stays in one place."
> 2. (Unexpected) "I don't know. It's just round."
> 3. (No response) Child shrugs or looks away.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(delighted)* It spins but stays put — that is different from your bike! Your bike wheels roll you forward. Great thinking! One more to find."
> 2. "*(encouraging)* Being round is what counts! Round parts can spin or roll. Your bike wheels roll you forward. Does this one roll too? One more to go!"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle)* I see round parts on this one too. Your bike wheels roll you down the street. Does this round part move the same way? One more find!"
>
> **Screen**: Photo slides into slot 3. Counter reads "2 of 3 found!" Comparison sparkle between the new find and the bicycle photo.

> **Round 3**
>
> *(Child takes a photo — e.g., a trash can lid, a scooter, or a toy car)*
>
> **AI says**: "*(thrilled tone)* One more round thing! What did you spot? Show me where the circle or wheel is."
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "A scooter! It has two wheels like my bike!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "A flower. It's round like a circle."
> 3. (No response) Child holds up the device without speaking.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(amazed)* Two wheels, just like your bicycle! Scooters and bikes both roll on wheels. Your collection is complete, Wheel Spotter!"
> 2. "*(warm, validating)* A flower — great eye! Its shape is round like a wheel. Nature has circles too! Your collection is complete!"
> 3. "*(cheerful)* I can see something with a round part! It looks like it could roll or spin. Your collection is complete, Wheel Spotter!"
>
> **Screen**: Final photo slides into slot 4. All slots filled. "Collection Complete!" banner with confetti animation.

> **STUCK BRANCH** (if child cannot find something after searching)
>
> **AI says**: "*(helpful, reassuring)* Hmm, round things can hide! Try the garage — look for wagon wheels or scooter wheels. Or check near the trash cans — even lids are round. You could also look at a garden hose reel or toy car."
>
> **Screen**: A hint map appears showing illustrated icons of common round-part objects (wagon, scooter, trash can lid, toy car) with dotted arrows pointing to typical locations (garage, sidewalk, yard).

**Step 4: Collection Complete + Synthesis**

> **AI says**: "*(proud, reflective tone)* Look at all your finds! Your bicycle has wheels with spokes. Now look at everything you found. Can you pick one that is most like your bike and one that is most different?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "The scooter is most like my bike. The lid is most different because it does not roll."
> 2. (Unexpected) "They are all round!"
> 3. (No response) Child looks at the collection grid silently.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(impressed)* The scooter rolls like your bike, and the lid just sits there. Some round parts roll you places, some spin in place, and some just cover things. You really thought about that!"
> 2. "*(enthusiastic)* Yes, they are all round! Some round parts roll, some spin, and some just sit still. Why do you think some roll and some do not?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(warm prompt)* Look at your bike's wheels and then look at each find. Which one rolls the most like your bike?"
>
> **Screen**: All four photos displayed in a grid. The bicycle photo is in the center with lines connecting it to each find. A "most alike / most different" toggle highlight appears as the child speaks.

**Step 5: Discovery Celebration**

> **AI says**: "*(warm celebration)* Amazing work, Wheel Spotter! You found three things with round parts, just like your bicycle. Some roll you places, some spin in place, and some just sit. Why do you think so many things use round shapes?"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) "Because round things roll! They move easy."
> 2. (Unexpected) "I don't know. Circles are just cool."
> 3. (No response) Child smiles or fidgets.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(delighted)* That is right! Round shapes roll and move smoothly. That is why bikes, wagons, and scooters all use wheels. You figured that out all on your own!"
> 2. "*(warm)* Circles are really cool! And here is a secret — round shapes are so good at rolling that people put them on almost everything that needs to move. Pretty clever, right?"
> 3. *(wait 2 seconds)* "*(gentle)* Round shapes are special. They roll! That is why your bike has wheels and not squares. You discovered something important today."
>
> **Screen**: Collection displayed as a cohesive set, photos arranged in a wheel pattern around the bicycle photo. Animated golden connection lines pulse between items. A "Why round?" thought bubble floats above.

**Step 6: Closing + IB Concepts**

> **AI says**: "*(proud, celebratory)* You are an amazing Wheel Spotter! You found round parts everywhere — on bikes, wagons, and more. You discovered the Function of wheels — round parts help things roll and move! And you showed Responsibility by looking carefully and thinking about how each one works. You earned your Wheel Spotter badge!"
>
> **Possible child responses**:
> 1. (Ideal) Child cheers or says "I did it!"
> 2. (Unexpected) "Can I find more wheels?"
> 3. (No response) Child watches the badge animation.
>
> **AI follow-up**:
> 1. "*(joyful)* You sure did! Keep spotting wheels wherever you go. You are a true Wheel Spotter!"
> 2. "*(enthusiastic)* You can always keep spotting! Every time you see something round that rolls, you are using your Wheel Spotter skills."
> 3. "*(warm)* Look at that shiny badge! You earned it by finding and thinking about wheels. Great job today!"
>
> **Screen**: "Wheel Spotter" badge animation — a magnifying glass with a spinning wheel inside, surrounded by the child's four collection photos as insets. The words "Function" and "Responsibility" appear artistically below the badge, styled with gear and wheel motifs. Gentle confetti and sparkle effects.
